Learning, memory, & selective attention

How does experience change attention?

The ability to select some relevant information from our environment while ignoring other distracting information is an important aspect of cognition. Whether you are focused on the conversation you're having in a crowded, noisy room and ignoring the (more interesting) conversation next to you, or driving down a busy street paying particular attention to the cars around you and ignoring all of the flashing storefront signs, it is clearly very important to be able to filter out those distractions and focus on the task at hand.

Often however, we encounter the same environmental contexts repeatedly and those regularities can provide important information about how attention should be applied. In this project, we examine how our ability to ignore distracting information changes with experience and how we learn about and use environmental regularities to help guide attention.

Attention & music perception

Do you hear what I hear?

Our experience of music is rich, complex, and complete. When we listen to a piece of music we hear all the individual parts - the guitar, bass, drums, and vocals - yet they all come together to form a singular experience. This seemingly effortless exercise, requires a series cognitive processes that segregate, identify, and organize auditory sources to construct a global representation of our experience. In this project, we investigate how attention shapes our experience of music. Namely, how focusing our attention on different aspects of the musical scene can shape the organization of sounds and how limitations in attentional capacity affect our global representations of music.

Brosowsky, N.P., & Mondor, T.A. (2016), Multistable perception of ambiguous melodies and the role of musical expertise. The Journal of the Acoustical Society of America, 140(2), 866-877.


Detecting concealed attitudes

Are concealed attitudes embedded in our language production?

There is considerable evidence that people hold implicit attitudes that may conflict with their explicit or expressed beliefs and influence their actions in measurable ways. In particular, implicit or suppressed attitudes can produce uncontrollable behavioral tells in laboratory tasks (IAT). In this project, we ask whether these tells are infused into measurements of typewritten language production when people are engaged in the act of persuasion. That is, are typist's true attitudes about their written statements embedded in the structure of their language production and are they detectable? We leverage computational models of language, language production measures (i.e., records of keystroke dynamics), and machine learning tools to investigate.

Brosowsky, N.P. & Crump, M.J.C. (November, 2018), You should hate this movie! Detecting concealed attitudes of online persuaders. Poster presented at the 59th annual meeting of the Psychonomic Society, New Orleans, LA.
